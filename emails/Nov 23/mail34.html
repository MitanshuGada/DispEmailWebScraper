xIn two scoreless cup finals against Chelsea, Liverpool had a high number of relatively low-quality shots, but couldn’t break through. In Paris, they seemed to come out of the blocks with a determination to create clear chances from counter-pressing. There’s a famous Klopp
quote from his Borussia Dortmund days about how no playmaker in the world is better than a counter-pressing situation, and the first quarter of the final felt like a return to first principles
of Klopp-ball.

Liverpool’s initial pressing scheme worked excellently but Madrid made a crucial change around 20 minutes, when Luka Modric dropped back into a double pivot with Kroos, behind
Casemiro, effectively inverting Real’s usual midfield. Real began to play through the press more easily. They had shown tactical resilience, and landed a psychological blow. Liverpool
put a lot – forethought, intensity, effort – into whipping up an early storm, but Real weathered it.

Real very successfully stopped Liverpool getting clear shots, not just in that first period but over the match as a whole. They defended in a low, compact block, keeping plenty of bodies in the box, denying Liverpool space in those central shooting locations, and it worked. They blocked nine shots, and Liverpool’s average xG per shot was 0.10 (in other words, a 10% scoring chance), lower than their average this season of 0.13.

Overall, Real’s performance was somewhat reminiscent of Didier Deschamps’s France. They lined up in an asymmetric 4-3-3, with a midfielder on the wing – Fede Valverde, playing the Blaise Matuidi role – who would drop back to form a 4-4-2 in the defensive phase. This shape was narrow and compact, allowing Liverpool little space between the lines.
