In our last email, we covered some of the foundations to cultivate your Talent Density Mindset. All so you can build an incredible team that will grow your business — while in flow, for you.

There are five steps to this game:

  1. Correctly Defining The Role
  2. Making Your Job Description Your Best Sales Page
  3. Using Talent Dense Hiring Channels
  4. Interviewing To Sell, Not To Assess
  5. Assessing Fit Through Discovery Projects

Let’s tear each apart so that by the end of this email you’re equipped to implement the whole stack.

Step 1—Correctly Defining The Role

This part of the process is about asking; "What gap in the business do I need to fill, what role fills this gap and what kind of person do I need to fill this role?"

Business Gap —> Role Definition —> Ideal Person For Role

Most people screw this up right out of the gate.

They make one of a few critical mistakes:

Mistake 1—Packing too many responsibilities/requirements into one role

The more requirements you put in a role, the harder you make it to hire for. Never try and hire a unicorn.

Make your role definition incredibly simple. Ideally distilled down to one thing that the candidate needs to do and be good at.

You’re probably thinking; "But I desperately need them to do all these other things".

That’s fine, but for the purposes of recruitment, force yourself to define the role by one or two crystal clear responsibilities.

Then find someone amazing at that one thing and soon you’ll be able to expand them out to other tasks.

The golden rule:

—> Recruit for a generalized role and you’ll hire no-one. Recruit for a specialized role and you’ll find someone great who you can generalize later.

Mistake 2—Blending skills that rarely go together in one-person

If you need someone technical, don’t structure a role where they also need to be good with people.

If you need someone who’s good at sales, don’t make it necessary for them to also be good at building processes!

Mistake 3—Hiring For A "No Man’s Land" Mid-Level Role

A lot of people hire for mid-level roles. But these are the toughest to hire for.

Go for super simple singular roles or for super elite advanced six-figure roles.

The singular roles are much easier to recruit for the reasons mentioned above.

And at the super high-level, paradoxically, it’s easier to hire a $250K person than a $50K person. Finding them is simpler as there are less options so you have to go direct.

Assessing them is easy because if they’re not almost immediately delivering incredible value they’re not worth the spend.

Whereas an $80K-ish role that is sort of senior but not quite is tough. Tough to define and recruit for, tough to assess performance once they’ve been landed.

Once you’re clear on the role and you’ve made it simpler than feels comfortable... then write out a Job Scorecard for that role; describing the vision for the role, what makes the ideal candidate an A-player the role.

Step 2—Making Your Job Description Your Best Sales Page

Alright, the role is defined and you’ve avoided the above three mistakes.

Now it’s time to write the Job Description with the most compelling sales copy you can possibly muster.

Use this format for the Job Description landing page:

  1. Title (Make titles simple and aligned to other titles in the market)
  2. Question (A question that calls out your ideal candidate and emphasizes the best thing about the role; E.g. "Would you like to run a world-class client success team with all the autonomy you could dream of in a mission-driven, rapidly scaling startup?")
  3. Our Company (This is where you put a compelling breakdown of your company, culture, vision, and mission, make it feel like they're getting on a rocket ship)
  4. The Role (Describe the role briefly and clearly in two or three compelling sentences, include details about pay range and location, etc.)
  5. This Is For You If… (List all of the unique descriptors of the ideal person that in a way that your candidate will resonate with; E.g. "This is for you if you are uniquely obsessed with client success and get a thrill from resolving client issues so well that they’re overjoyed")
  6. Qualifications… (Here you write out the qualifications, make it clear but engaging, dodge corporate speak; E.g. "You’ve managed 3+ client success reps before and know how to recruit incredible reps single-handedly")
  7. Team Testimonials (Here you want to put testimonials from other members of your team describing why working with you is amazing)
  8. Client Testimonials (Here you want client testimonials to demonstrate the positive impact you’re having on others via your mission)

Finally, you’ll want to add a compelling video to the top of the landing page that is about 3 minutes long and further sells the role, summarizing what's on the page.

Boom. You’ve got a stellar Job Description that sells the role 10X better than anyone else out there.

Step 3—Using Talent Dense Hiring Channels

To start, a list of common hiring channels, ranked from best to worst:

  1. Referrals — From other A-player employees or your network
  2. Deep Outreach — Reaching out to those in your network and asking "Who’s the best person you know at X, and would you mind introducing me?", doing that continuously until you hit someone who’s a fit
  3. Outbound Headhunting — Building a list of possible candidates via LinkedIn and getting in touch with each through custom outreach
  4. First Degree Inbound — Marketing the job to your list, social, audience or following
  5. Recruiters/Sourcers — Sourcers find candidates and you screen them, recruiters find candidates and screen them for you, both can work
  6. LinkedIn Ads — Posting a job ad on LinkedIn
  7. Job Boards — AngelList is a solid choice

You’ll notice that the top three—Referrals, Deep Outreach & Outbound Headhunting—are time intensive and fairly challenging to scale.

Correct. Recruiting is messy, it takes time, it’s difficult to systematize. But those channels are the best, by far.

Some tips to nail each of these top channels:

Referrals — Offer a huge cash bonus for a successful referral. I like to offer up to $30,000 in cash. The condition is that they have to be a direct referral and the candidate has to stay in the role for three months. This is win-win because it drives up people's willingness to refer and you only pay it if you’re sure the candidate is a rockstar. At which point it’s worth it.

Deep Outreach — When asking for intros, don’t ask "Who do you know that's good at X and interested in a job?". If you do that, people will have no intros for you because good people they know aren’t looking. Instead ask; "Who do you know that’s the best at X, would you mind connecting me with them, even if they’re not looking for a job?".

Outbound Headhunting — First, get someone on your team to build a list of 100 or so candidates that look like rockstars for the role on LinkedIn. Then in-mail all of them, with a brief description of your company, the role and the comp––making it all sound incredible of course. Now, the trick; don’t ask "Is this of interest to you?", instead ask "Do you know anyone this would be of interest to?". The majority of the time they’ll reply saying "Well, actually, it’s of interest to me!".

A critical point on using these channels for recruitment; you may get drastically lower volume than you’re used to.

When I was bad at recruiting I would get 100+ applications per role.

Now that I’m good at recruiting I’ll usually have under 10 (sometimes under 5) candidates per role before making the hire. Quality > Quantity.  

Step 4—Interviewing To Sell, Not To Assess

Laszlo Bock, infamous ex-SVP of People Operations at Google says, "Interviews are a terrible predictor of performance."

He’s right — don’t use interviews to ASSESS a candidate, use interviews to SELL a candidate.

As you start to get potential candidates from the above channels, begin to book calls with them.

In some cases, it will be explicit that it’s an interview, but in many cases the purpose of the call can be "just to chat". That’s because many of these candidates won’t even be looking for a role.

On that "interview sales call" ask them about their dream job, 5-year career goals, and desire for contribution. Learn everything you can about what they want, what motivates them, and what they don’t like.

Then pitch your company around what they want and don’t want.

This may take a few calls to do successfully.

The next step after these calls is getting them to agree to do a paid "Discovery Project". It’s in this "Discovery Project" where you’ll assess whether they’re actually a good fit.

Which brings us to the final step:

Step 5—Assessing Fit Through Discovery Projects

At this point, hopefully the interview sales call has gone well enough that a candidate is interested in "the next step".

The next step is a "Discovery Project" where you pay them a small stipend to do a day or two of work that simulates the role.

Tell the candidate that this allows them to get a flavor of the role and you to get a sense for how they think and work.

When designing the discovery project, keep it simple and closely tied to the role.

Examples:

  • If you’re hiring a salesperson, have the discovery project be three mock sales calls and the writing of a follow-up email
  • If you’re hiring a marketing leader, have the discovery project be an analysis of all of your marketing efforts with a proposed 30-day plan for how to half Customer Acquisition Cost

In some cases, especially for higher level roles, you’ll want multiple discovery projects.

Often, with a six-figure role I’ll do:

1 Day Discovery Project —> 1 Month Discovery Period —> Full-Time Offer

But for shorter roles this isn’t necessary.
