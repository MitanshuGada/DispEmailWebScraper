Big Tom,

In 2007, I was a trainer for the Canadian Forces.

One day, a new group of recruits came in. The usual bunch: 20-ish, males, not so fit. But this woman stood out.

A 4 feet 10, 46-years-old Romanian woman with a PhD in anthropology and a last name I couldn't pronounce. She spoke with a thick accent. And promptly failed her fitness test.

Women had to do 9 push-ups. She did 4. My job was to help her do at least 9. Or else she would be kicked out.

A few weeks in, I learned she enrolled as a last resort. She couldn't find work in Canada, and needed to help her family back in Romania.

I'm not sure she knew what to expect. Basic military training is hard, even for men in their 20s. A few don't make it through. But for a women aged 46?

Training was taking it's toll on her. After a month, she came up to me. I noticed how tired and stressed out she looked. She was about to get tested again. She asked me:

"Do you think I will pass the push-up test?"

I said:

"That depends on who carries out the test. You don't always go all the way down on your push-ups. Your arm has to be at least parallel to the floor, or your push-ups won't count."

Failing the test meant she might get kicked out, fail to find work, and not be able to support her family.

The day of the test, I was surprised to find out I would be carrying out the test for her. Usually, we didn't test the recruits we trained.

I tested a few males first. They did well. Her turn came. She nodded and got into position. I counted:

"1, 2, 3, lower..."

"Lower" meant her push-up wasn't low enough, and didn't count. She kept going:

"4, 5, lower, lower..."

She paused at the top and took a deep breath. That was allowed.

"6, 7... lower... lower..."

Another pause.

"8... lower... lower... 9..."

She looked up, surprised. I said: "Keep going!"

She ended up doing 13 push-ups. We both knew that meant she passed the test. And she would be able to move forward in the military and ultimately help her family out.

She got up, crying silently. Of relief, I guessed. She nodded and walked back to her position. There was no talking during tests. Later, when I gave her her report, she said:

"I will pray for you."

I remember it to this day. I don't know how she ended up, but I do know she improved a lot in just one month. In an environment that can break even young men.

Her secret? I can only guess, but I'd say: dedication. She must have done everything right: training, eating, and recovery.

Her secret can be yours too. With enough dedication, you can succeed against tough odds. Like this 46-yo Romanian soldier.
