Weekend Briefing

From Reuters Daily Briefing

By Robert MacMillan, Reuters.com Weekend Editor

Hello and welcome to the Weekend Briefing. Let’s jump right in with the latest news on Israel and Gaza, developments in Ukraine and an episode of the World News podcast on Britain and slavery that you will not want to miss.

Hostages abducted by Hamas gunmen are handed over to the International Red Cross. Hamas Military Wing/Handout via REUTERS

Israel expects more hostages to be freed

  • The latest: Hamas was expected to release a second group of Israelis as a planned four-day truce to allow an exchange of 50 hostages for Palestinian prisoners held in the Gaza Strip holds. Egyptian security sources said they received the names of 14 Israeli women and children from Hamas and were waiting to learn when the hostages would be handed over to Egyptian authorities. Reuters spoke with families whose relatives came home - as well as those who still wait.
  • Moving north: Many Palestinians emerged from makeshift shelters at the start of the ceasefire to begin the journey back to their homes, despite Israel dropping leaflets warning them not to return to an area it described as still being a dangerous war zone.

Zelenskiy names 'three victories' Ukraine needs

  • International front: President Volodymyr Zelenskiy said Ukraine needed three wins beyond the immediate war with Russia, including the approval of major aid packages from the U.S. Congress and European Union, and a formal start of accession talks to join the EU.
  • On the ground: Ukrainian forces meanwhile staved off unrelenting Russian attacks on the devastated city of Avdiivka as winter looms, and Kyiv said it is preparing troop-mobilization reforms without providing details on what they are. The capital suffered what officials said was Russia's largest drone attack of the war, leaving five people wounded as the rumble of air defenses and explosions woke residents at dawn on Saturday.

Europe's far-right gathers in Portugal

  • Left behind: Still in a celebratory mood after a shock win for Dutch populist Geert Wilders, some European far-right leaders gathered in Lisbon to support Portuguese counterpart André Ventura ahead of a snap election in March. The gathering came days after Wilders, beating all predictions, booked major gains in the Dutch election on an anti-immigration platform.
  • In Argentina: While Wilders might have to soften some of his more extreme and anti-Muslim stances to form a government, Argentina's libertarian President-elect Javier Milei said closing the central bank remains non-negotiable. Milei nevertheless will have to work closely with the more mainstream conservative bloc, whose public backing was key to his winning the run-off election last week.

Keep it cheap this year
  • Economizing: Shoppers this U.S. holiday season are hot for merchandise priced below $35, setting the stage for the cheapest shopping period in years following the Thanksgiving holiday. Black Friday appeared to be more subdued than usual this year across the U.S. and Europe.
  • Strike! Amazon workers walked out at multiple locations across Europe in protests against the U.S. e-commerce giant's working practices on Black Friday.

Before I forget…

 

The Crown, the cabinet and the UK's legacy of slavery: At the start of the 19th century, a British politician grew rich from slavery. His descendants include King Charles III, as well as the man with the keys to the nation's treasury. The Reuters World News podcast examines how we investigated these links and their long moral shadow and implications for the idea of reparations.

 

Irish Prime Minister Leo Varadkar said it was safe to return to Dublin city center after police arrested 34 people following rioting that was triggered by the stabbing of three young children in the street. Police have not given a motive for the stabbing, nor have they commented on the attacker's nationality.

 

Russia must stop sending asylum seekers across its frontier into Finland in what amounts to a "hybrid attack," Finnish Prime Minister Petteri Orpo said, after it temporarily shut all border passenger crossings bar one.

 

Sam Altman's return as OpenAI's chief executive will strengthen his grip on the startup and may leave the ChatGPT creator with fewer checks on his power. Ahead of his brief exile, several staff researchers wrote a letter to the board, warning of a powerful artificial-intelligence discovery that they said could threaten humanity.

 

Derek Chauvin, the former Minneapolis police officer convicted in the death of George Floyd, was stabbed in federal prison and seriously injured, the Associated Press reported.

 

South Korea is moving toward a ban on dog meat.  Many of those involved in the centuries-old practice, including farmers and restaurateurs, are fighting to keep it legal.
