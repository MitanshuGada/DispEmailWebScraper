Today, I’d like to try a little experiment with you.

Because there’s nothing quite like a demonstration to really try things out for yourself.

I’m going to show you 2 versions of the same chord… a regular old F-major chord… just 3 notes… F–A–C.

Then, I’m going to ask you to play those 3 notes in a different position.

The result is quite different. But I want to make sure you can really hear the difference. So I’ll draw your attention to a few things while we play this alternate version of the F-major chord.

Okay, first of all, head over to your piano and play a regular old F-major chord above middle C.

It looks like this…

Normal sounding chord, right? 3 basic notes. Easy to find. Comfortable in the hand. Etc.

Okay, now, I’d like you to move down a little to the left of middle C and play the same 3 notes… F–A–C.

But… play them in this order.

First, with the little finger on your left hand, play a low-ish F (see image below).

Then, with the same hand, using your thumb, play the C that’s 5 notes above the F.

Lastly, with your right hand, play the A that’s just a couple notes below middle C.

Okay, so that’s 3 notes in total. This is where you can find them on the keyboard…

Okay, try one more time. Put your left hand little finger on F, your left thumb on C, and your right hand on A.

Then, play all 3 notes together.

Okay, good job!

Now, hop back up the keyboard and play the original F-major chord we started with.

What’s different about it?

They’re the same notes. But what’s the difference?

Well, first of all, the 2nd version of the F-major chord is a little lower on the keyboard which is a much warmer register on the piano.

But notice how far apart those 3 notes are in the 2nd version of the chord?

The bottom two notes are 5 steps away from each other. And the middle and top note are 6 steps apart from each other.

Just by spreading out the 3 notes of a normal F-major chord… creating more space between notes… the chord sounds more majestic and resonant.

Try it again and see if you can really hear the difference.

I bet you can.

This change of chord position… from the basic F–A–C… which we call “closed” position… changes the sound quite a lot.

There’s a warmth to the wider-spread 'open' chord.

And this is a perfect example of the difference between PLUNKY and MAJESTIC chords.

One is not harder than the other to play.

It’s simply voiced in such a way that it unlocks the rounder, more resonant qualities of the piano’s acoustics.
